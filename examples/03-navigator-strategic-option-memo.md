## 1. The Question

Should ABC Refinery commit ~USD 1.4 bn of own/sponsor capital to execute the Full Brownfield Upgrade ("Option A"), or pivot to a Toll-and-Trader partnership with a Tier-1 integrated counterparty ("Option B"), in which approximately 75% of the capex is borne by the partner in exchange for 25–40% of the integrated Gross Refining Margin?

The question matters because the two paths produce very different ABC Refinery futures. Option A preserves brand sovereignty and full GRM upside; Option B exchanges some of that upside for materially lower balance-sheet exposure and faster access to a Tier-1 partner's market reach and technology. Neither is obviously dominant.

---

## 2. Option A — Full Brownfield Upgrade

| Dimension | Value |
|---|---|
| Total capex envelope | ~USD 1.4 bn |
| Financing structure | Sponsor equity + BRP-21 deemed-duty + Sukuk/conventional debt + JV partner (3-pillar waterfall) |
| Year-5 EBITDA (base case) | USD 220 M / yr |
| NPV at 12% discount (base case) | USD 380 M |
| IRR (base case) | 26% |
| Discounted payback | ~5.5 years |

**Strengths:** Full GRM capture; brand sovereignty; full optionality on petrochemicals integration (Pathway A bundled in EPC envelope); aligned with regional precedent (Reliance Jamnagar, SABIC integration).

**Weaknesses:** Full balance-sheet exposure to delayed-transition or demand-destruction scenarios; concentration risk on a single mega-project; FBR risk on the BRP-21 deemed-duty regime (modelled separately under FBR Risk scenario).

---

## 3. Option B — Toll-and-Trader Partnership

| Dimension | Value |
|---|---|
| ABC Refinery capex contribution | ~USD 350 M (≈25%) |
| Partner capex contribution | ~USD 1,050 M (≈75%) |
| GRM share to partner | 25–40% (sliding scale on integrated margin) |
| Year-5 EBITDA (base case, ABC share) | USD 130 M / yr |
| NPV at 12% (base case) | USD 195 M |
| IRR (base case) | 19% |
| Discounted payback | ~4.8 years (capital-efficient) |

**Strengths:** Balance-sheet preservation; access to partner's market reach and technology; faster path to mechanical completion through partner project-management muscle.

**Weaknesses:** Permanent 25–40% GRM giveaway; loss of brand sovereignty; partnership governance complexity; dependence on partner's strategic appetite (which may evolve).

---

## 4. Why the Hybrid Wins

Neither pure option is dominant across the scenarios that matter. Under the "current policies + utilisation strong" scenario (~60% probability-weighted), Option A is clearly superior. Under "delayed transition + utilisation weak" (~30%), Option B is materially better. Under "net-zero + utilisation collapse" (~10%), neither is great but Option B's lower capex exposure makes it the lower-regret choice.

The **hybrid strategy** captures Option A's upside while preserving Option B's optionality:

1. **Proceed with Option A at FID.** Build the full brownfield upgrade. Full GRM exposure to the base + bull cases.

2. **Negotiate in parallel a non-binding Heads of Terms Agreement ("HoTA")** with a Tier-1 partner (ADNOC, SABIC, Sinopec, Reliance) that crystallises the option to convert from owner-operator to toll-processor, on either of two pre-defined triggers:
   - **Utilisation trigger:** average refinery utilisation < 65% for 4 consecutive quarters by FY30; OR
   - **Carbon trigger:** Pakistan formally announces a carbon price > USD 50/tCO₂e before FY32.

3. **Value the embedded option** using a Black-Scholes-style real-options model: volatility ~35%, tenor 6 years, exercise cost ~USD 180 M (restructuring + partner equity dilution). The option value falls in the USD 28–34 M range — material, but small relative to Option A's NPV. This is the cost of the insurance.

The embedded option is contractually mapped (not implicit) — the HoTA pre-defines the partner, the triggers, the conversion mechanics, and the GRM-share schedule. Activation under either trigger is a Board decision, not a partner unilateral.

---

## 5. Why This Differs From "Just Decide A or B"

The temptation in Board discussions of this kind is to force a binary. Real options thinking — applied properly — shows that the *most valuable structure* is often neither pure option but a pre-architected pivot path that maintains directional commitment (Option A) while pre-purchasing the right to defect (Option B) if the world changes. The HoTA is the legal instrument that converts "defection" from a panic-driven 2030 renegotiation into a pre-modelled, pre-priced, pre-governed option exercise.

This is the pattern that distinguished SATORP (Aramco-Total) from many other downstream JVs of the era: the JV structure was the pivot path, not the destination.

---

## 6. Recommendation

The Board is asked to:

(a) **Approve Option A** as the recommended FID at the upcoming Board sitting;

(b) **Authorise the CEO and CFO** to negotiate a non-binding HoTA with one of three short-listed Tier-1 partners, targeting signing by FID + 12 months;

(c) **Embed the trigger architecture** in the HoTA as defined above (utilisation < 65% for 4 consecutive quarters by FY30 OR Pakistan carbon price > USD 50/tCO₂e by FY32);

(d) **Mandate quarterly Board reporting** on the embedded option's status (trigger metrics + partner relationship) through the BSCC and the Board Strategy Committee.
