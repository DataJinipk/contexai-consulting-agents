## 1. Governance

### 1.1 Board-level oversight

The Board of Directors of ABC Refinery Pk Limited has overall responsibility for the company's climate-related risks and opportunities, exercised through the Board Sustainability Climate Committee ("BSCC") constituted under S192 of the Companies Act 2017 and Regulation 25 of the Listed Companies (Code of Corporate Governance) Regulations 2019. The BSCC is composed of three non-executive directors (including the Chair), the CEO ex-officio, and reports to the Board on a quarterly cadence.

The BSCC's remit specifically includes:
- Approval of the company's climate-related risk register, including scenario analysis assumptions and time-horizon definitions;
- Approval of the company's Scope 1, 2 and 3 greenhouse gas inventory boundaries and quantification methodology, consistent with the GHG Protocol;
- Approval of the company's transition plan and any material amendments thereto;
- Review of climate-related performance against published targets;
- Oversight of the climate-related disclosures prepared under IFRS S2 and any voluntary frameworks (CDP, TCFD legacy disclosures).

### 1.2 Management-level oversight

Day-to-day climate-related management is exercised through the Climate Risk Steering Group, chaired by the COO and including the VP Refining, VP Trading, VP Finance, Head of HSE, and Head of Investor Relations. The Steering Group meets monthly during disclosure-cycle months (Q1, Q2 of each fiscal year) and quarterly otherwise.

## 2. Strategy

### 2.1 Identified climate-related risks and opportunities

Risks and opportunities have been identified across three time horizons: short-term (≤2 years, aligned with the operating planning horizon); medium-term (2–10 years, aligned with the brownfield upgrade economic horizon); and long-term (>10 years, aligned with the structural transition window for downstream refining in this region).

Physical risks include: acute risks from coastal storm-surge intensification affecting marine terminal availability; chronic risks from rising mean sea level and seawater temperature affecting cooling-water system thermal margin.

Transition risks include: carbon-pricing risk (Pakistan currently has no domestic carbon price but has signalled IFC-aligned policy alignment by 2030); demand-destruction risk for road fuels under aggressive EV-penetration scenarios; product-specification risk (Euro-V mandate already legislated; Euro-VI under discussion); financing-cost risk from sectoral exclusions emerging at multilateral development banks.

Climate-related opportunities include: refinery-petrochemical integration improving structural EBITDA stability as fuels demand peaks; advanced-recycling feedstock opportunities arising from the Pakistan municipal solid-waste sector; voluntary-market carbon-credit revenue from on-site flare-gas recovery and methane abatement.

### 2.2 Scenario analysis — three NGFS pathways

ABC Refinery has conducted scenario analysis aligned with the NGFS scenario framework (Phase IV vintage). Three scenarios are tested for financial and operational implications:

| Scenario | Description | Carbon-price 2030 USD/tCO₂e | Refinery utilisation FY30 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Current Policies | National policies as of mid-2025; minimal incremental action | 0 | 80% |
| Delayed Transition | Late but sharp policy action; carbon-pricing arriving by 2030 | 35 | 76% |
| Net Zero 2050 | Aggressive immediate action; full decarbonisation pathway | 70 | 65% |

Under the Net Zero 2050 scenario, the brownfield upgrade NPV remains positive in the base case (because the upgrade improves yield + product spec compliance) but the embedded Toll-Processor real option becomes valuable as utilisation falls below the 65% threshold. Under Delayed Transition, the upgrade NPV is robust through the analysis period.

### 2.3 Resilience of strategy

Three structural moves preserve resilience across all three scenarios: (a) the embedded Toll-Processor real option preserves a pivot path if utilisation collapses; (b) the petrochemicals integration thesis (Pathway A bundled, Pathway B optional) diversifies revenue away from pure fuels; (c) crude supplier diversification de-risks single-counterparty exposure that becomes acute under transition scenarios.

## 3. Risk Management

### 3.1 Process for identification, assessment, and monitoring

Climate-related risks are identified through an annual board-approved risk-identification workshop and supplemented by:
- Industry-wide horizon scanning (sectoral reports, peer disclosures, regulator publications);
- Internal operational monitoring (HSE incident database, NEQS compliance tracking, methane LDAR audits);
- Counterparty risk reviews on supply, off-take and financing arrangements.

Each identified risk is scored on a 5×5 likelihood × impact matrix; the risk register is reviewed quarterly by the Climate Risk Steering Group and presented annually to the BSCC for re-approval.

### 3.2 Integration into enterprise risk management

The climate risk register is a sub-register of the company's master Enterprise Risk Management framework. Tier-1 climate risks (combined L×I score ≥12) flow upward into the master register and are reviewed by the Board Audit and Risk Committee in addition to the BSCC.

## 4. Metrics and Targets

### 4.1 Scope 1, 2 and 3 inventory

Scope 1 inventory follows the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard and is independently verified each fiscal year. Scope 1 sources include fired heaters, gas turbines, flares, fugitive emissions, and the on-site captive cogeneration plant. Scope 2 uses the market-based method for grid imports during downtime windows.

Scope 3 categories most material to ABC Refinery's footprint:
- **Category 11 — Use of sold products**: dominant category (>85% of total footprint); calculated from product volumes × IPCC default emission factors;
- **Category 4 — Upstream transportation and distribution**: maritime + pipeline + rail;
- **Category 1 — Purchased goods and services**: crude oil and major catalyst purchases.

### 4.2 Methane disclosure (OGMP 2.0 alignment)

ABC Refinery has committed to OGMP 2.0 Level 3 reporting by end-FY27 (using EPA Method 21 + tier-3 source-level site-specific emission factors) and Level 4 by end-FY29 (with QOGI optical-gas-imaging quantification). This commitment is reflected in the methane-LDAR programme and the OE Roadmap's NEQS sustenance budget.

### 4.3 Targets

| Target | Metric | Baseline | FY30 | FY40 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Scope 1 absolute reduction | tCO₂e/yr | FY25 audited | -15% | -45% |
| Methane intensity | kg CH₄/t throughput | FY25 audited | -50% | -85% |
| Energy intensity (Solomon EII®) | EII index | ~118 | ≤95 | ≤88 |
| Scope 3 Cat 11 absolute | tCO₂e/yr | FY25 audited | -8% | -30% |

Scope 1 targets are linked to the brownfield upgrade's energy-efficiency uplift and the OE Roadmap's furnace/heat-integration programme; methane targets are linked to the OGMP 2.0 commitment; Scope 3 Cat 11 reductions are linked to the petrochemicals integration thesis (Pathway A+B).
