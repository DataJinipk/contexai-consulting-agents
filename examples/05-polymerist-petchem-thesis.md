## The Strategic Question

Pakistan is a chronic polymer importer. The country consumes ~950 KTA of polypropylene (PP) and ~1.05 MTA of polyethylene (PE), of which ~85% of PP and ~95% of PE is imported — a combined polymer import bill of meaningful USD bn/year in normal-price years. No domestic refinery currently has integrated polymer capability. The strategic question for ABC Refinery is whether — and at what scale — to step into this gap as the brownfield upgrade is executed.

---

## Three Pathways Evaluated

This memo evaluates three discrete integration pathways and ranks them on five dimensions: capex intensity, IRR at base-case netbacks, strategic optionality, execution complexity, and ESG positioning.

| Pathway | Capex (USD M) | Polymer output (KTA) | IRR (base) | NPV @ 12% (USD M) | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A** — FCC propylene splitter + 90 KTA PP (bundled in brownfield) | 180–230 | 90 PP | **22%** | 75 | ✓ Build inside upgrade EPC |
| **B** — On-purpose propylene via PDH + 350 KTA PP (Year-3 follow-on) | 850–1,050 | 350 PP | **19%** | 265 | ✓ Commit at FID Year-3 |
| **C** — Full naphtha steam cracker (1 MTA C2-equivalent) | 3,600–4,200 | 950 (C2+C3+aroms) | 13% | 180 | ✗ Reject — scale mismatch |

The recommended portfolio is **A + B**, sequenced. Combined A+B output ~440 KTA of domestic PP — about 46% of national PP demand — at integrated capex USD 1.0–1.3 bn (additional to the brownfield), with blended IRR of 20% and combined NPV of USD 340 M.

---

## Why PDH (and Why Not MTO/MTP)?

We evaluated MTO (methanol-to-olefins) and MTP (methanol-to-propylene) as alternative on-purpose propylene routes — and rejected both for ABC Refinery:

| Route | Propylene yield | Feedstock risk | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| PDH (Oleflex / CATOFIN) | Single-product propylene (>85% selectivity) | LPG/propane price risk (manageable; Saudi Aramco term basis) | ✓ Recommended |
| MTO (UOP/Hydro) | Mixed C2/C3 ~1.0–1.4 C3/C2 ratio | Methanol import dependency; no domestic source | ✗ Reject |
| MTP (Lurgi) | Higher C3 selectivity than MTO | Methanol import dependency; logistics + ESG risk | ✗ Reject |

Pakistan has no commercial-scale methanol production. The MTO/MTP route would require either imported methanol or first building a methanol plant — adding USD 700+ M of incremental capex and a dependency on natural gas allocation that Pakistan's industrial sector cannot guarantee. PDH is materially the simpler and lower-risk choice.

---

## Why Pathway C Is Rejected (for now)

Pathway C — a full 1 MTA ethylene-equivalent naphtha steam cracker with downstream PE (HDPE + LLDPE), PP, and BTX aromatics — is the most ambitious option (the "Reliance Jamnagar scaled-down" playbook). We rejected it for the FY26-FY30 decision window for four reasons:

1. **Capex incompatibility** — USD 3.6–4.2 bn is ~3× the brownfield upgrade itself; would require sovereign sponsor or sub-IG financing;
2. **Scale-feedstock mismatch** — 1 MTA C2 needs ~3 MTA naphtha; ABC Refinery's post-upgrade naphtha production is ~700 KTA. Would need 2.3 MTA imported naphtha versus today's net export position;
3. **Polymer portfolio over-extension** — adding PE, BTX, and aromatics simultaneously is operationally untested for ABC Refinery; learning-curve risk is multiplicative not additive;
4. **Strategic match** — cracker investments anchor regional petchem hubs (Singapore, Jubail, Daesan); ABC Refinery's site is a coastal refinery, not a hub — economics don't align.

Pathway C is the right move for ABC Refinery at some point in the 2035-2040 horizon — most likely as a JV with a Tier-1 partner where ABC Refinery contributes land, infrastructure and operational know-how while the partner brings cracker capex and specialty-resin technology. For the FY26-FY30 window, Pathway C is premature.

## Integrated Margin Economics (Pathway B base case)

| Line item | USD/t PP |
|---|---|
| PP CFR Pakistan reference (Platts / ICIS) | 1,180 |
| Less: Pelletisation + bagging cost | (35) |
| Less: Variable conversion (utilities, catalyst) | (120) |
| Less: Fixed conversion (incl. depreciation) | (180) |
| EBITDA margin per tonne PP | 845 |
| Less: Propylene feedstock (PDH netback) | (720) |
| **NET PP-over-propylene spread** | **125** |
| Plus: PDH margin (propylene over propane) | 230 |
| **INTEGRATED MARGIN per tonne PP** | **355** |

Integrated margin of USD 355/t at 350 KTA = USD 124 M/yr EBITDA at steady state from Pathway B alone. Combined with Pathway A (~USD 25 M/yr at integrated USD 280/t): **USD 149 M/yr** EBITDA at base case from the petchem portfolio.

## Partnership Architecture

Pathway A can be self-funded inside the brownfield envelope. Pathway B should be partnered — for balance-sheet relief AND for technology + market-access reasons. Four credible partner archetypes:

| Partner archetype | Example | What they bring | What they want |
|---|---|---|---|
| Middle-East petchem major | SABIC, ADNOC, OQ | Technology, capex (60-70%), polymer market access | Off-take rights, possibly JV control |
| Asian integrated player | Reliance, Lotte Chemical | Operational know-how, capex (40-50%) | Pakistan market entry, learning |
| Specialty / converter partner | Borouge, Braskem, Inéos | Polymer grade specialisation, brand access | Domestic supply security |
| Sovereign-aligned | SIFC-channelled GCC capital | Sovereign equity + soft loans | Strategic Pakistan presence |

Base-case recommendation: pursue a Middle-East partner (likely ADNOC given existing Pakistan engagement and Borouge specialty-resin footprint). Open dialogue should be initiated during pre-FEED (Q3 of brownfield Year-1) so a non-binding HoTA can be in place before Pathway B FID.
